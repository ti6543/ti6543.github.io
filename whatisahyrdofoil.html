What is a hydrofoil?

A hydrofoil is a wing shape that produces lift (and drag) when passing through water:

The amount of lift it creates is dependent on three things:

  • Size of the foil
  • Speed of the foil through the water
  • Lift Coefficient - (related to the Angle of Attack of the foil)

Size

A bigger foil will provide more lift than a smaller one.

Speed

A hydrofoil produces more lift at faster speeds. Lift is proportional to the square of the speed, so going twice as fast (without changing Angle of Attack) results in four times as much lift.

Lift Coefficient

The Lift Coefficient tells us how lift changes with Angle of Attack

The Angle of Attack is the angle between the foil and the incoming water flow:

As Angle of Attack increases, more lift is produced. However, there is a limit to this. At an Angle of Attack of between 10 and 20 degrees (depending on the foil), it can no longer produce more lift with increasing Angle of Attack, and it starts decreasing. This is know as a stall.

Controlling the foil

We always need the foil to be producing the correct amount of lift, equal to the weight of the rider. This needs to be at a range of speeds. Too little lift and we sink back down to the water, whilst too much results in us flying up into the air (breaching).

The rider controls the amount of lift by varying the Angle of Attack. At slow speeds a large Angle of Attack is required, and at high speeds a low Angle of Attack. So as a foil accelerates, the rider is required to lower the nose of the board to maintain constant lift.

Slow - high Angle of Attack

Fast - low Angle of Attack

Because lift increases as the square of speed, controlling the foil by adjusting Angle of Attack becomes much harder at high speed (for the foil), as the adjustments to Angle of Attack become much finer. Using a smaller foil makes controlling higher speeds easier.
