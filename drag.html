Drag

Foils produce drag when going through the water. There are two types of drag that affect foils:

  1. Parasitic drag - this is the drag caused by the resistance of the water. It increases as the square of the speed.
  2. Induced drag - this is the drag cause by creating lift, which induces vortices. This is bigger at high Angles of Attack (slow speeds)

Total drag is the combination of the two:

From the graph we can see that there is a moderate speed at which drag is at a minimum. We call this Minimum-Drag speed. This speed is the most efficient and requires the least amount of power from the sail/wing or wave. If you are doing a low power activity like pump-foiling, you will want to do it at this speed.

Stable and unstable speed

We call the plot of total drag against speed the "drag curve":

Because of the shape of the drag curve, we can break it down into two regions:

  1. SPEED UNSTABLE - here, any decrease in speed increases drag. This could be from some chop, or a wind lull. This results in a further loss of speed, which again increases drag, leading to a stall. (The only way to recover from this is a big increase in power)
  2. SPEED STABLE - here, a decrease in speed reduces drag, so less power is needed to maintain the new speed. When up on the foil we always want to be riding in the speed stable region. At these speeds, any small reduction in power and/or speed will result in a stable lower speed.

Lift:drag ratio and Takeoff

An important foil characteristic is it's lift:drag ratio. That is how much drag it ends up creating per unit of lift

This graph shows how lift:drag ratio changes with Angle of Attack, when up on the foil

At very high speeds Angle of Attack is low, but parasitic drag is very high

At Minimum-Drag speed, we are at L/d max, the maximum lift per unit of drag

At low speeds we are at higher Angles of attack and induced drag is high

Takeoff

In order to takeoff, we need enough lift to counter the weight of the rider

Takeoff involves

High power situation

With lots of power, takeoff is easy. We accelerate to a fast speed whilst still on the water, ending up at a speed above minimum-drag. By this point we are creating enough lift to get out of the water, and we rise up. The speed in in the stable region.

Low power

With low power, things become a lot harder.

From the lift equation, to takeoff we need a combination of:

  • Size
  • Lift Coefficient
  • Speed

Size - Bigger foils create more lift, so a bigger foil will require less speed to foil
