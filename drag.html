Drag

Foils produce drag when going through the water. There are two types of drag that affect foils:

1. Parasitic drag

This is the drag caused by an object ploughing through water. A bigger frontal area (cross-section) of the foil will increase this, so thick large foils will have more than small thin ones. It increases as the square of the speed.

2. Induced drag

This is the drag caused by creating lift, which induces vortices. Creating lift means that there is a low pressure area above the foil, and a high pressure area beneath. At the wingtips, the high pressure water tries to move to the low pressure area. This circular motion of water extends backward behind the foil, and causes drag.

This effect is bigger at high Angles of Attack (slow speeds):

Drag is high at both very low speeds, and very high speeds.

There is a moderate speed at which drag is at a minimum. We call this Minimum-Drag speed. This speed is the most efficient and requires the least amount of power from the sail/wing or wave. If you are doing a low power activity like pump-foiling, you will want to do it at this speed.

Stable and unstable speed

We call the plot of total drag against speed the "drag curve":

Because of the shape of the drag curve, we can break it down into two regions:

  1. SPEED UNSTABLE - here, any decrease in speed increases drag. This could be from some chop, or a wind lull. This results in a further loss of speed, which again increases drag, leading to a stall. (The only way to recover from this is a big increase in power). These speeds should always be avoided.
  2. SPEED STABLE - here, a decrease in speed reduces drag, so less power is needed to maintain the new speed. When up on the foil we always want to be riding in the speed stable region. At these speeds, any small reduction in power and/or speed will result in a stable lower speed.

Lift:drag ratio

An important foil characteristic is it's lift:drag ratio. That is how much drag it ends up creating per unit of lift

This tells us how efficient a foil is.

This graph shows how lift:drag ratio changes with Angle of Attack

The peak of the curve is where we produce the most lift per unit of drag. It's also the same as the minimum-drag speed we saw earlier.

More efficient (High Aspect, thin) foils have better lift/drag ratios. More on this later.
