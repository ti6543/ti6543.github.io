How a foil is controlled

On this page we'll look at how a foil is controlled by the rider

We saw on the last page that the rider needs to control both the overall upward lift, and the nose up/down forces from the foil (and its drag).

We'll look at how the foil changes the Balance Point at different speeds. First a recap.

Recap: Overall Upward Lift

Without any rider correction, lift increases with speed

In order to compensate for changing speed, the Angle of Attack is adjusted. At low speeds, we therefore need a high Angle of Attack. At higher speeds, the Angle of Attack is reduced.

Slow speed and high Angle of Attack

High speed and low Angle of Attack

Recap: Rotational forces

My moving the bodyweight, the rider can control the NOSE UP/DOWN rotation

When the bodyweight is in the correct position, known as the Balance Point, there is no NOSE UP/DOWN rotation

The effect of speed on the balance point

The Balance Point of the setup will change with speed.

This means that the rider has to shift their bodyweight to match the changing Balance Point

The change of Balance Point is dictated by the stabiliser, the drag, plus any changes in the lift position of the front foil:

Stabiliser effect

We've just seen that the front foil will have a lower Angle of Attack at increased speed.

However, because the stabiliser is upside down, any decrease in Angle of Attack on the front wing INCREASES the Angle of Attack on the stabiliser.

This, combined with extra speed (squared), increases the downward lift. This creates an extra nose up rotation.

The foil setup (fuselage length, stabiliser size, shim) will affect how strong this effect is. More on this later.

Drag pitch down effect:

As mentioned on the previous page, the drag of the foil creates a nose down rotation, the opposite effect to the stabiliser.

This increases with the square of speed.

This opposes the stabiliser. The stabiliser increase at high speed is larger however, due to the effect of both speed and Angle of Attack

Centre of pressure changes:

The centre of pressure (CofP) of the front foil is where the lift comes from.

As the Angle of Attack changes on the front foil, the centre of lift changes.

The centre of lift moves forward at low speeds. The effect of this is to increase the NOSE UP rotation force.

You have probably noticed that just before you stall a foil it tends to pitch the nose even higher.

The profile of the foil will determine how strong this effect is. More on this later

Total effect on the balance point:

  • Stabiliser - nose UP at higher speeds
  • Drag - nose DOWN at higher speeds
  • CofP - nose UP at lower speeds, especially near the stall.

The type of foil and the setup will determine exactly how the balance point changes at low and high speed.

For example, a setup with a big stabiliser might induce nose UP at high speed

However a foil that has a backward CofP effect at higher speeds combined with a smaller stabiliser might be nose DOWN at high speed

Summary

  • The rider controls overall upward lift by adjusting Angle of Attack, and NOSE UP/DOWN rotation by moving their bodyweight forward or back
  • If the rider is ahead of the Balance Point the nose sinks, if behind the nose rises
  • The Balance Point can change with speed, caused by the changes in stabiliser lift, drag and Centre of Pressure movement on the front foil. The rider must shift their bodyweight to compensate
