Choosing a foil

From the previous page, you should now have a good idea of the properties a foil can have:

  • Size in cm2

      Bigger
    • Lower takeoff speed required
    • Able to maintain lift at slower speeds
    • Better for heavier riders
    • Better for beginners
      Smaller
    • Faster top speeds, and more control at high speed
    • Better turning

  • Thickness

      Thicker
    • More lift
    • Easier to control
      Thinner
    • Faster
    • Slightly better glide

  • Aspect Ratio

      High Aspect
    • More lift
    • Less drag
    • Better glide
      Low Aspect
    • Easier to control
    • Better turning

What foil or foils you choose is going to be based on:

  1. Your ability level
  2. Your activity
  3. Power available - wind strength, wave steepness etc
  4. Waves/Swell - the type of waves and swell you are riding

Beginners

Beginners will want an easy to use, easy to lift foil. So big, thick and low to mid Aspect. More confident beginners may look to go more toward the thinner mid-Aspect foils, that they can keep and use as a light wind foil when they are more advanced.

A rough guide would be 1500cm2 for very lightweight, up to 2000cm2+ for heavyweights (90kg)+

Intermediates

Once learning to gybe and starting to ride swell, a medium sized Mid Aspect foil will suit these riders

Stepping down to 1200-1500cm2 will allow faster riding, whilst maintaining decent low end

Advanced

Advanced riders will start to look for foils that suit their dicipline and likely conditions

Racing/speed

Here, the smallest thin high Aspect foil will get the best speeds. The problem will be getting up onto the foil. This may require a longer board with a bit more volume to help get onto the foil.

Freestyle - jumping

For this we want good speed and pop, but also control.

A small-medium sized foil, Mid-Aspect with moderate thickness would work well. Some are designed with a CofP movement that coupled with a slightly bigger stabiliser gives NOSE UP at higher speeds to aid jumping (pop).

This NOSE UP at higher speeds can make it more difficult to use at faster speeds on waves.

Light Wind

To get foiling in a low power scenario (e.g. light wind winging, shallow swell), we need lift at lower speeds

From the lift equation with low speed, we need to increase S (size) and/or cL (Lift coefficient)

There are a number of solutions to this, all with their drawbacks:

  1. Mid Aspect, Large area - downside is slow speeds
  2. High Aspect, Large area - Good for lulls, downside is difficulty turning, slow speeds
  3. Thick High Aspect, Small/Medium area - this is a good solution (my favourite). You can make the foils less than 1metre wide for reasonable turning. Top speeds will be limited by thickness.

Swell and Waves

Choosing a foil to use in swell/waves will make a big difference to how you ride

Wave speed and steepness

When riding waves, we need to match the foil speed range to the wave speed. How fast do waves travel? The answer depends on whether we are talking about ocean swell, or breaking waves

Unbroken swell

Swell in deep water travels at 1.56*period in knots. So weak windswell (short period) is going around 10kts, but proper groundswell (long period) will be going at 20ks+

If you're downwinding, or riding swell out to sea, these are the speeds you'll be interested in

When downwinding, you'll be using swell that is soft, so low drag is important

So ideally, we need a foil that:

  • Has low drag to cope with flat areas - high Aspect.
  • Thinner will help reduce drag at higher speeds (groundswell)
  • Thicker or Bigger will allow lower speeds (windswell)

There is an issue with using small foils - getting them onto the foil. Modern Downwind SUPfoil board shapes are overcoming this by creating board bottoms that are easier to get foiling, and allow smaller foils to be used. Wingfoiling will be ok provided there is sufficient wind.

We're not that bothered with really hard turning, so don't need to worry so much about big wingspans. High Aspect Foils are perfect for this.

Breaking waves

When waves start getting toward shallow water they slow down, and eventually break. Their speed will reduce, down to 6*Squareroot(depth in metres)

  • 3ft (peak to trough) waves will break in 4ft of water, and have a minimum speed of 6kts
  • 6ft (peak to trough) waves will break in 8ft of water, and be minimum speed of 8.5kts

These are minimum speeds. Before the wave breaks, which is often where the best foiling sections are, will be going faster (especially with higher period swells)

Better foilers won't be travelling straight with the wave, so can easily double these speeds when riding.

Riders need to be able to cope with the acceleration that comes with going down steep bits of wave. This is both in terms of finer AoA adjustments, and balance point movements.

So ideally, we need a foil that:

  • Has a fairly low stall/min-drag speed, if waves are smaller
  • Turns well
  • Extra drag helps reduce acceleration and high speed control issues. This can come from reduced Aspect Ratio, and also thickness.
  • Smaller foils reduce high speed control issues in bigger waves

Again, smaller foils mean better control, and better turning, but we need to be able to get them going in the first place. Light winds and sizeable waves give a real conundrum as to whether to prioritse ease of foiling versus ease of riding.

It's my opinion that Mid Aspect foils are better for breaking waves than High Aspect.

Flat water pumping

For this, we need the least drag per unit of lift. This means a High Aspect foil.

Slower is better for us, because there is drag from the rider going through the air that scales as the square of speed. This means a big foil for lowest drag and best endurance.
